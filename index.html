Cloud capture tools get switched off.
A folder you own doesn't.

Your captures shouldn't depend on someone else's server staying online — or someone else's business staying solvent.

In December 2025, Rewind — a popular always-on capture app — was acquired by Meta and shut down.

People who had trusted a cloud service with years of their screen history were left scrambling. That's the risk with anything that lives on someone else's servers: it can be bought, paywalled, or simply turned off.

Screenlapse takes the opposite bet. It writes plain image files to a folder you own — your Mac, or your own NAS. There's no server to retire, no account to suspend, no subscription to lapse. If Screenlapse vanished tomorrow, every frame you've captured would still be sitting exactly where you put it.

    Allow Screen Recording

    On first launch, Screenlapse's welcome window asks for Screen Recording. Click Allow, then Open System Settings, and switch Screenlapse on under Privacy & Security → Screen Recording. Quit and reopen Screenlapse once so macOS applies it.

  3. 3

    Allow direct capture

    When macOS asks Screenlapse to “bypass the private window picker”, click Allow. That lets Screenlapse capture your whole screen on schedule, without you choosing a window each time. It's expected — and only Screenlapse is doing it.

    “Screenlapse” wants to bypass the window picker
    …to directly access your screen.
    Allow
    Open System Settings
  4. 4

    Choose where captures go

    Open Settings → Destination and point Screenlapse at your folder — a NAS share like smb://nas.local/Screenshots, or any local folder. Done: it now captures on your schedule and delivers every frame, quietly from the menu bar.

Questions, answered.

The things people ask before they download.

Is it a subscription?
No. Screenlapse is a one-time purchase — pay once and it's yours, including updates. There's nothing to renew and no recurring charge.
Where do my screenshots go?
To a folder you choose — a local folder on your Mac or a NAS share like smb://nas.local/Screenshots. They're saved as standard HEIC, JPEG or PNG files you can open, move and back up anywhere.
Does it use the cloud?
No. There's no account, no cloud service and no telemetry. Nothing leaves your Mac except the frames it writes to the destination you picked. Screenlapse can't see your captures, and neither can we.
Will it slow down my Mac?
No. It's a lightweight menu-bar app that writes frames locally in the background and uploads them on its own schedule. HEIC keeps files small, and an optional resolution cap and free-disk floor keep it gentle on your storage.
What happens after the 14-day trial?
You'll have tried every feature, free. To keep capturing, you buy Screenlapse once — $19 at launch ($29 afterwards), no subscription. Your existing captures are plain files in your folder, so they're always yours regardless.
